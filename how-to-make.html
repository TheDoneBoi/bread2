From flour to loaf

How to Make Bread

The ancient art of bread-making distilled into clear, achievable steps. All you need is flour, water, salt, yeast — and a little patience.

What You Need

The Essential Ingredients

🌾

Flour

Bread flour (high protein, 12-14%) creates the best gluten network. All-purpose flour works too, with slightly less chew. For a 1-loaf recipe, use 500g (3¾ cups).

💧

Water

Use lukewarm water (about 105°F / 40°C) to activate the yeast. Too hot kills it, too cold makes it sluggish. You'll need 325ml (1⅓ cups).

🧂

Salt

Salt strengthens gluten, controls fermentation, and develops flavor. Without it, bread tastes flat and rises too fast. Use 10g (2 tsp).

🫧

Yeast

Instant (fast-acting) yeast is beginner-friendly — no proofing needed. Active dry yeast works too but dissolve it in warm water first. Use 7g (1 packet).

Step by Step

The Bread-Making Process

1

Mix the Dough

In a large bowl, whisk together the flour, salt, and yeast. Create a well in the center and pour in your lukewarm water. Use a wooden spoon or your hands to stir until a shaggy, rough dough forms — it doesn't need to be smooth yet. Let it rest for 10 minutes (this is called autolyse and lets the flour absorb the water fully).

2

Knead

Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Push it away from you with the heel of your hand, fold it back over itself, rotate 90°, and repeat. Knead for 8-10 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky (not sticky). The windowpane test: stretch a small piece thin — if you can see light through it without it tearing, your gluten is developed.

3

First Rise (Bulk Fermentation)

Place the dough in a lightly oiled bowl, cover with a damp towel or plastic wrap, and let it rise in a warm spot (around 75°F / 24°C) for 1 to 1½ hours — until it has roughly doubled in size. This is when the yeast does its magic, producing CO₂ gas and alcohol that give bread its flavor and airy structure.

4

Shape the Loaf

Gently punch down the risen dough to release excess gas. Turn it out onto your work surface and shape it into a round boule (tuck the edges underneath and rotate to create surface tension) or an oval batard. Place the shaped loaf seam-side down on a parchment-lined baking sheet or in a floured proofing basket.

5

Second Rise (Proofing)

Cover and let the shaped loaf rise for 30-45 minutes. Meanwhile, preheat your oven to 450°F (230°C). If you have a Dutch oven, place it inside to preheat too — the enclosed steam creates an incredible crust. The dough is ready when it springs back slowly when poked with a finger.

6

Score & Bake

Use a sharp razor blade or knife to slash the top of the loaf — this controls where the bread expands (called oven spring) and looks beautiful. Bake for 25-30 minutes until the crust is deep golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom. Internal temperature should read 200-210°F (93-99°C).

7

Cool & Enjoy

This is the hardest step: let the bread cool on a wire rack for at least 30 minutes before slicing. Cutting into hot bread releases steam that the crumb needs to set properly — slice too early and you'll get a gummy interior. Once cooled, slice, admire that crumb, and enjoy the fruits of your labor.

Level Up

Pro Baker Tips

🌡️

Temperature is Everything

A kitchen thermometer is your best friend. Water temp, dough temp, and oven temp all make or break the final bread.

Essential
⚖️

Weigh, Don't Measure

Use a digital kitchen scale. "1 cup of flour" can vary by 30% depending on how you scoop it — grams are precise and consistent.

Game Changer
💨

Steam = Crust

A Dutch oven traps steam around the bread, creating a blistered, crackly crust. No Dutch oven? Place a pan of water on the oven floor.

Pro Tip
🕐

Cold Fermentation

Shape your loaf, then proof overnight in the fridge (12-18 hours). The slow, cold rise develops deeper, more complex flavors.

Advanced
🫱

Stretch & Fold

Instead of traditional kneading, do 4 sets of stretch-and-folds during bulk fermentation. It builds gluten gently with less effort.

Technique
📓

Keep a Bread Journal

Record your flour types, hydration %, times, temperatures, and results. Bread baking is scientific — tracking helps you improve.
